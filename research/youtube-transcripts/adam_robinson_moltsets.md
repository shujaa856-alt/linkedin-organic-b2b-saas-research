## Summary

Adam Robinson presents LinkedIn writing as a repeatable process rather than a matter of waiting for inspiration. His workflow begins with choosing topics that have enough relevance and interest to justify a post. He then treats the opening hook as a distinct component whose job is to earn attention and move the reader into the argument. The body develops the selected idea, while the close gives the post a deliberate ending instead of allowing it to fade out. By breaking creation into these stages, Robinson makes high-performing content easier to analyze and improve. The overall lesson is that large reach is more likely when topic selection and post construction are handled systematically.

## Key Insights

- Topic selection is a separate strategic decision and should happen before drafting.
- The hook must earn enough attention for the rest of the post to be read.
- The body should develop one central idea rather than dilute it across competing points.
- A purposeful close gives the reader a clear final impression or next step.
- Treating posts as a structured system makes performance easier to review and improve.

## Relevance to LinkedIn Organic Strategy

B2B SaaS marketers can turn Robinson's process into a simple editorial framework: identify a buyer-relevant topic, craft several possible hooks, build a focused body, and finish with a deliberate close. This is especially useful for founder-led and subject-matter-expert content because it separates valuable expertise from the mechanics needed to package it effectively. Teams can review performance by component, asking whether weak results came from the subject, opening, development, or ending instead of labeling the entire post a failure. Reusing a consistent structure also reduces production friction and makes it easier to publish regularly without making every post sound identical. Over time, documented observations about which topics and constructions resonate can become an evidence-based LinkedIn playbook for the company.
