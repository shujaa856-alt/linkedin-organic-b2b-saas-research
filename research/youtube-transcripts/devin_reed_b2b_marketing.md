# Devin Reed – YouTube Research

## Video

**Title:** The #1 mistake brands make with B2B creators (Aneesh Lal, Wishly)

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9i-4lp4Y8d4

**Published:** September 17, 2025

## Summary

Devin Reed interviews Wishly founder Aneesh Lal about how B2B brands can work more effectively with professional creators. The discussion explains that creator campaigns often disappoint because marketers treat them as isolated experiments instead of making a sufficiently committed, well-designed investment. Selecting a creator requires more than looking at audience size; the creator's relevance to the ideal customer profile, credibility, and ability to influence that audience are more important. The episode also examines how effective partnerships are structured so that brands receive useful commercial value while creators have the support and incentives to produce strong work. Lal's experience building a large B2B creator agency illustrates how demand for specialized representation has grown alongside the creator economy. The broader argument is that successful creator marketing depends on strategic fit, thoughtful qualification, and long-term relationship building rather than opportunistic sponsorships.

## Key Insights

- Small, undercommitted pilot campaigns can create misleading conclusions about whether creator marketing works.
- Creator selection should begin with audience and ideal-customer alignment, not follower totals alone.
- Credibility, consistency, and the ability to move an audience are stronger signals than superficial reach.
- Brand–creator agreements work best when expectations, incentives, and deliverables are valuable to both sides.
- Long-term partnerships give creators more context and give brands a better chance to build cumulative trust.

## Relevance to LinkedIn Organic Strategy

For B2B SaaS companies, the episode suggests treating LinkedIn creators as trusted distribution partners rather than rented media inventory. Marketers should identify practitioners whose expertise, audience, and point of view closely match the product's ideal customers, then collaborate on material that fits the creator's established voice. The same principle applies to a company's own organic program: credibility and audience relevance matter more than maximizing follower counts or publishing volume. Sustained relationships can also generate a connected series of posts, conversations, and proof points that compound familiarity over time. Teams should evaluate these efforts through qualified engagement, audience response, and downstream commercial signals instead of judging a single post in isolation.
